Cebu

Cebu is where the Philippines you know begins. The Santo Niño was given here, the cross was planted here, the Spanish century started here. Most of what came after, came after.

In April 1521, Ferdinand Magellan’s expedition reached the harbour at what is now Cebu City. Within a week he had baptised eight hundred locals, given Queen Juana of Cebu a small wooden image of the Christ child as a baptismal gift, and planted a wooden cross on the shore. Seven days later he was killed by Lapu-Lapu’s warriors on Mactan, twelve kilometres east. The expedition sailed home without him. Forty-four years later, in 1565, Miguel López de Legazpi returned, founded the first Spanish settlement in the country, and the wooden Christ child given to Queen Juana was rediscovered, intact, in the burned remains of a hut. It is still there. The trip is built around going to see it.

You stay in central Cebu City at Rublin Hotel, walking distance to the Basilica del Santo Niño. Day 1 is arrival. Day 3 is breakfast and the airport. Day 2 is a full city day: the Basilica in the morning, Magellan’s Cross beside it, the Cebu Heritage Monument at the old plaza, Fort San Pedro on the harbour, and Taboan dried-fish market. In the afternoon, the trip moves up into the hills above the city: Sirao Garden, the Temple of Leah, the 10,000 Roses Café, and the CCLEX bridge for sunset. The morning is what the trip is actually for. The afternoon is the rest of the day.

After Palawan, what Cebu offers is a city built on something. Not a beach. Not a landscape. A foundational moment that gave the country its name, its religion, its first colonial century. The basilica that holds the Santo Niño is the most-visited Catholic church in the Philippines; millions come every year, and most of them are Filipino. You will not be the only foreigner there, but you will be one of the very few.

The Santo Niño, in glass

The Santo Niño is small. About thirty centimetres tall. Carved in Flanders sometime before 1500, given to Queen Juana of Cebu in April 1521, lost when the village burned, rediscovered in March 1565 by a Spanish soldier picking through the ash. The figure is dressed now in heavy embroidered robes, with a crown, and stands behind glass in a side chapel of the basilica. The chapel is busy. There is a queue most days. People light candles in the courtyard outside, in long ranks of red and yellow flames, and pass the figure asking for help with whatever they came to ask about. You can stand and watch this for as long as you want; nobody is timing the foreigners. The relic itself, behind the glass, is unremarkable to look at. The remarkableness is that it is still there, and that the queue has not ended in five hundred years.

Magellan’s cross, in a kiosk

Twenty metres from the basilica entrance, in a separate octagonal kiosk on the main road, is the cross Magellan’s expedition planted. The original wood is encased inside a hollow tindalo cross, which means what you can see is a darker wooden shape inside a lighter wooden frame. The encasement is for protection. Pilgrims used to chip pieces off the original to take home as relics, and there was, by the late 1700s, very little cross left. The kiosk has a painted ceiling depicting the planting itself, faded, repainted twice. The cross is not theologically important and is not, strictly speaking, a relic. It is significant because it is where Magellan’s expedition stood five hundred years ago. The geography is the meaning.

Fort San Pedro at the edge of the city

Fort San Pedro is the smallest Spanish military fort in the Philippines, built in 1565 by Legazpi’s company, finished in stone over the next century. The walls are thick. The triangular footprint encloses about two thousand square metres. Inside, there is a small museum, a courtyard, and a row of cannons. The garden inside the walls is well kept. The fort sits on the harbour, at what was once the working edge of the city, and is now slightly shadowed by the modern port behind it. You climb the walls and look out toward Mactan. Lapu-Lapu killed Magellan on the far shore of that water, in waist-deep surf, with the expedition’s longboats unable to reach him in time. The Filipino who killed Magellan has a city named after him now, just across the bridge.

The days

  1. Day 01

    Arrival

    Landing at Mactan-Cebu airport, transfer to Rublin Hotel in central Cebu City. Around 45 minutes by van depending on bridge traffic. Standard check-in from 2pm. Afternoon free; the Basilica is a 15-minute walk if you’d rather get the first visit out of the way and return later for a quieter morning.

  2. Day 02

    City and uphill

    A full day. Morning in the heritage core: Basilica del Santo Niño, Magellan’s Cross, the Cebu Heritage Monument, Fort San Pedro, Taboan dried-fish market. Afternoon up into the hills above the city: Sirao Garden, Temple of Leah, 10,000 Roses Café, the CCLEX bridge for sunset. Drop-off at SM Cebu City; own way back to the hotel.

  3. Day 03

    Out

    Breakfast at the hotel, then check-out (standard 12pm). Free time until the airport transfer. Standard transfer to Mactan-Cebu for departure.

When to come
December through May is dry and the light is best for the heritage walking. June through October is wet season; the tour still runs but the open-air stops feel different. The Sinulog festival (third weekend of January) is the country’s largest religious procession; the city doubles in population that week. Beautiful and crowded; book months ahead.
Worth knowing
The Basilica is technically open daily but quieter on weekday mornings. Photography is allowed in the courtyard but not in the chapel where the Santo Niño stands.
