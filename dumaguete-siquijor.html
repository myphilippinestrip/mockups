Dumaguete & Siquijor

Four days, two islands, one ferry. The pace is the point.

Dumaguete is a university town on the southern tip of Negros, and a quiet one for somewhere that has been growing steadily for thirty years. The Rizal Boulevard runs along the seafront for about a kilometre. From the boulevard, in the morning, you can see Cebu and Siquijor on a clear day, and the ferry to Siquijor leaves twice in the early hours. The trip uses Dumaguete as the base. You sleep there for three nights and travel out in the morning to Siquijor, to Apo Island, and on the last day, into the Valencia uplands.

Siquijor itself is a small island of about ninety-five thousand people, ringed by a single road that takes most of a day to drive properly. It has a reputation, partly earned and partly cultivated, for folk healing and mysticism. The reputation makes for striking signage and for tourist stops you can choose to take seriously or not. What is genuinely there, at no need for embellishment, is a 400-year-old balete tree with a freshwater pool at its base, a Spanish-era church in Lazi, and a stretch of beach at Paliton that holds up against any in the Visayas.

After Palawan, what this trip offers is a different kind of slow. Not beach-resort slow, not honeymoon slow. Coastal university town in the morning, ferry across a strait at midday, mystic-leaning small island in the afternoon, sea turtles the next day, volcanic uplands on the way out. The geographic spread does the editorial work; the brand of slowness is gentle, varied, and particular to this corner of the country.

The boulevard at 6:40

At 6:40 on a Tuesday, the Rizal Boulevard in Dumaguete is already busy. The joggers are finishing, the tricycle drivers are starting, and the 7:00 ferry to Siquijor is loading at the pier just south of the boulevard. You can sit at one of the cafés that open early (Sans Rival is the famous one, slightly tourist now but still doing the silvanas they have always done) and watch the ferry leave with whoever caught the early run. The view across the strait, on a clear morning, is Siquijor itself. From the boulevard it looks closer than it is; the ferry is forty-five minutes by fast craft. The slowness of the boulevard and the faster boat to the island is the rhythm of the trip.

The balete tree, Siquijor

The Old Enchanted Balete Tree in Lazi is genuinely four hundred years old, give or take, and is a banyan rather than a balete in the strict botanical sense, but the local naming is what counts. At the base of the tree is a small freshwater pool fed by spring water, and in the pool live small fish that nibble dead skin from the feet of anyone who sits on the edge with their feet in the water. The fish spa is, technically, a tourist attraction. The tree itself is something else: roots dropping like ropes from twenty metres up, the trunk wider than three people standing arm-to-arm, bark that has been touched smooth by hands across centuries. You will not believe the age of the tree until you see it. You will believe it after.

Apo Island, with the turtles

Apo Island is a small volcanic outcrop forty-five minutes by boat from Dumaguete, a fishing community of about a thousand people, and one of the oldest community-managed marine reserves in the Philippines. Since 1985, locals have worked the protected zones themselves, and the result is a turtle population dense enough that nearly every snorkeller sees one within the first ten minutes. The water is clear, the reef shelves quickly, and the green sea turtles are unbothered by swimmers who keep a respectful distance. There is also, on a half-day visit, time to climb the short trail to the lighthouse view deck. From the deck the island is laid out below you, the channels visible, the boats pulling in for lunch.

The days

  1. Day 01

    Arrival

    Landing at Dumaguete airport, transfer to Coco Grande in the city centre. Around fifteen minutes by van. Afternoon Dumaguete city tour: Silliman University, the Rizal Boulevard, the cathedral and bell tower. On your own this evening; the boulevard is good walking after dark.

  2. Day 02

    Siquijor

    A full day on Siquijor. Early ferry from Dumaguete, then a circumnavigation of the island anti-clockwise: Paliton Beach, Pitogo Cliff, the Enchanted Balete Tree at Lazi, Cambugahay Falls, Salagdoong Beach, the Triad Café viewpoint. Back to Dumaguete by evening ferry.

  3. Day 03

    Apo Island

    Early boat from Dumaguete to Apo Island. Snorkelling in the marine reserve with sea turtles, a short trek to the lighthouse view deck, lunch on the island. Back to the city in the afternoon. Free evening.

  4. Day 04

    Valencia and out

    Breakfast, then check-out and the Valencia upland tour: Cata-al War Museum, Tierra Alta Forest Camp, Sulfuric Mountain, Red Rock Hot Spring, and Pulang Bato Falls. Lunch in the uplands. Standard transfer to Dumaguete airport for departure.

When to come
March through May is dry and clear, the best months for Apo Island visibility. June through October is wet season; ferries to Siquijor occasionally cancel in heavy weather, and Apo Island is sometimes unreachable for a day or two. November through February is cool dry, comfortable, and the most reliable for the boat days.
Worth knowing
The package covers four days of fairly continuous activity. Day 4 ends with a same-day flight after the Valencia tour, which is tight; the driver-guide tracks your departure time and flexes the morning if needed. Apo Island swimming with sea turtles is the highlight for many; if you don’t snorkel, the boat trip is still worth it for the lighthouse view. The Siquijor Swing Tour covers the entire island in one day, which is a lot; we sometimes recommend a one-night stay on Siquijor for slower travel, arranged after booking.

Four days for this trip is calibrated. The fourth day is the Valencia uplands on the way to the airport, which is a useful contrast to the three coastal days that came before; sulphur, hot springs, freshwater pools, then your flight. We can extend with a Bohol leg (one ferry, two hours) or a slow stay on Apo Island in a small homestay, if either is the trip you’d rather have. Ask us.
