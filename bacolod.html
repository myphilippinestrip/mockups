Bacolod

Bacolod was built on sugar. Most of what you’ll come to see, the houses, the cathedrals, the long afternoon light, was paid for by it.

In the late nineteenth century, Negros Occidental was the wealthiest province in the Philippines per capita, and Bacolod was its capital. Sugar barons built mansions in the city and across the surrounding towns, Silay and Talisay, in a style that was part American colonial, part Italian Renaissance, part whatever the foreign architect they hired had in mind. Many of those houses are still standing. A few of them are open to the public, by appointment, which usually means phoning ahead. The trip is built around an unhurried day across the three cities, with the houses as anchors.

You stay at L’Fisher in the city centre, which is a hotel from a different era and is not pretending otherwise. From there, the tri-city loop runs anti-clockwise on Day 2, starting in Silay (the heritage town, museums in the morning), through Bacolod itself (the cathedrals, the plaza, the markers), and out to Talisay in the late afternoon for The Ruins. The driver moves at a pace that allows for actual looking. Most stops are between fifteen and thirty minutes. The Ruins is longer.

After Palawan, what Bacolod offers is a city, not an island. Streets, not coasts. Old houses you can walk into rather than landscapes you photograph from boats. The food is famously good. The light, in the afternoons, is warm in a way that flatters the limestone. You will leave understanding why the festival is called the City of Smiles, but the smiles are not the point of the trip; the houses are.

The Ruins, at five o’clock

The Ruins, in Talisay, is the skeleton of a mansion built in the early 1900s by Don Mariano Ledesma Lacson for his Portuguese wife, Maria Braga, who died in childbirth before the house was finished. He completed it anyway. It stood for forty years. In 1942, with Japanese forces advancing on the province, the United States Army Forces in the Far East asked Filipino civilians to destroy properties of likely strategic value rather than let them be occupied. The Lacson family burned their own house. The fire took three days. What remains is the ivory-stone superstructure, the columns and arches and pediments, with the entire interior gone. At five in the afternoon the limestone glows. You will hear someone in your group say it looks like an Italian villa, and they will be right.

A morning in Silay

Silay is fifteen kilometres north of Bacolod. In the morning, before the heat, you can walk a stretch of about six blocks and see four of the heritage houses without rushing. Hofileña Ancestral House is the one with the art collection, Filipino masters, mostly, from the Lacson family’s circle and beyond. Balay Negrense is the bigger museum, the former home of Don Victor Fernandez Gaston, sugar baron, with the rooms preserved roughly as they were lived in. Both houses have grandchildren who do the unlocking. You phone ahead. You take your shoes off at the door. You listen.

Inasal, at night

Chicken inasal is what Bacolod cooks better than anywhere else, and saying that anywhere else in the Philippines will start an argument. The marinade is calamansi, pepper, vinegar, and annatto, which is the colour. The chicken is grilled over coconut husk charcoal, which is the smoke. It is served with rice and a small dish of chicken oil mixed with soy and calamansi to drizzle. You will be asked which part you want; the answer most people don’t know to give is paa (leg). It is not part of the package, because almost nobody comes to Bacolod and eats inasal only at the hotel. We will tell you which place to go to. There are several. The good ones are in the side streets.

The days

  1. Day 01

    Arrival

    Landing at Bacolod-Silay airport, transfer to L’Fisher Hotel in central Bacolod. Around half an hour by van. Afternoon free to settle in or walk the plaza. On your own this evening; ask the driver-guide where to find inasal.

  2. Day 02

    Tri-city tour

    A full day across Silay, Bacolod, and Talisay. Morning in Silay for the heritage houses (Hofileña, Balay Negrense). Through Bacolod for the cathedrals, the Capitol Lagoon, and the city’s markers. Late afternoon at The Ruins in Talisay. Roughly 8am to 6pm with a midday break for lunch.

  3. Day 03

    Out

    Breakfast at the hotel, then check-out. Free time until the airport transfer. Standard transfer to Bacolod-Silay for departure.
