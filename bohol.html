Wonder / Green interior

Bohol

Bohol’s wonders are countryside wonders. The hills get the postcards. The river is the better part of the trip.

The Chocolate Hills are real, and they are roughly what the photographs show. What the photographs cannot show is that they are quietly strange to stand near. Twelve hundred conical limestone hills, give or take, covered in grass that turns brown in dry season and gives the place its name. The viewing deck at Carmen is a hundred steps up. You climb them. The hills are still there. They do not look like chocolate. They look like geometry, repeated patiently across an entire province.

The trip is built around one full day inland. You leave Panglao after breakfast, drive into the interior, and over the course of six or seven hours visit the hills, the tarsier conservation area at Corella, a man-made mahogany forest the road runs straight through, the Loboc river for lunch, and Baclayon Church, which has been working since 1596. Lunch on the river is a floating bamboo restaurant with a buffet and recorded music from speakers tied into the trees along the banks. The combination should not work, and somehow does.

After Palawan, what Bohol offers is a different argument for a Filipino trip. Land, not water. Interior, not coast. Things to look at that are not the ocean. The accommodation is on Panglao, near Alona, where the beach is fine and is not the point. The point is the day inland.

A morning at Carmen

The Chocolate Hills viewing deck sits a hundred steps above the road, which is enough to remember on the way back down. From the top, you see them. Not all twelve hundred, but a few hundred at least, repeating to the horizon. Geological consensus calls them eroded limestone, which is not the sentence you want while looking at them. Local consensus calls them the tears of a giant whose lover died. That is the better sentence, even if you don’t believe it.

Tarsiers, at Corella

The Philippine tarsier is the smallest primate in the world, or close enough. They are the size of a clenched fist. They live entirely on insects. They do not do well in captivity, which is why the conservation area at Corella is one of the few places you can reliably see them, asleep on a branch in the daylight, rotating their heads slowly when noise reaches them. The rule, posted at the entrance and enforced quietly by the wardens, is whisper. It is the correct rule. After ten minutes you will not want to whisper either. You will want to leave them alone.

Loboc, at midday

Lunch on the Loboc is a floating bamboo restaurant on a wide green river. It moves at the pace of a long meal. There is a buffet of countryside food: lechon, pancit, grilled fish, rice in three preparations. There is also, periodically, music from speakers tied to the trees along the banks, sometimes a guitarist on a small platform waving as the boat passes. The river itself, which is the actual reason to be there, is the colour of moss.

The days

  1. Day 01

    Arrival

    Landing at Tagbilaran airport, transfer to Panglao. Afternoon free to settle in, walk Alona Beach, or read depending on the flight time. On your own this evening.

  2. Day 02

    Inland

    A full day in the countryside. Chocolate Hills viewing deck at Carmen, the man-made mahogany forest, the tarsier conservation area at Corella, lunch on the Loboc river, and Baclayon Church. Back to Panglao by late afternoon.

  3. Day 03

    Out

    Breakfast at the hotel, then check-out. Free time in the morning depending on your flight. Transfer to Tagbilaran airport for departure.
